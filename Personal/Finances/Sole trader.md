### **Potential Tax Advantages as a Sole Trader**

1. **Claim Business Expenses**
You can deduct **legitimate business expenses** like:
    - Software subscriptions (e.g., GitHub, Figma)
    - Hardware (laptop, phone)
    - Internet and phone bills
    - Travel and accommodation (if business-related)
    - Home office expenses
    - Marketing, domain names, hosting
2. **GST Threshold (Optional Below $75k)**
If you're under **$75,000 revenue/year**, you can choose *not* to register for GST, which simplifies your obligations.
*(But in your case, you’ve registered for GST — so you must charge and report it.)*
3. **No Payroll Tax or Super for Yourself**
You don’t pay payroll tax or compulsory super for yourself (though you *can* make voluntary contributions and claim a deduction).
4. **Simplified Tax Reporting**
Business income is included in your **personal tax return** (as a separate business schedule), so:
    - You avoid company tax admin
    - Losses can offset other personal income (e.g., from a part-time job)

---

### ⚠️ **Limitations / Considerations**

5. **No Income Splitting**
All profits are taxed at your **personal marginal tax rate**. Once your income exceeds ~$45k, you're paying 32.5%, and 45% over $180k. Companies pay a flat 25-30%.
6. **No Fringe Benefits**
As a sole trader, you can’t access tax-effective benefits like salary packaging or fringe benefit tax (FBT) exemptions.
7. **Unlimited Liability**
You’re personally liable for business debts — no legal separation between you and the business.

---

### 🔁 **When to Consider a Company (Pty Ltd) Instead**

- You're earning **$120,000+ consistently**
- You want **limited liability protection**
- You need to **bring on co-founders, investors, or employees**
- You're reinvesting profits and want to **cap tax at 25–30%**
