## Tax Advantages of a Company (Pty Ltd)

### 1. **Lower Tax Rate on Profits**

- Companies pay a **flat tax rate**:
    - **25%** for base rate entities (most small companies)
    - **30%** for larger companies (rare at startup/freelancer stage)
- If your **personal income tax bracket is higher than 25%** (i.e., you earn over ~$45k), then putting profits into a company can save tax.

🧮 **Example:**

- $120k income as a sole trader → ~$31k tax
- $120k profit in company → $30k tax **max**, potentially less if you retain profits and pay yourself a salary/dividends strategically.

---

### 2. **Income Splitting with Family/Partners**

You can:

- Pay family members for work
- Issue dividends to shareholders (e.g., spouse, trust)
- Use **discretionary trusts** or **bucket companies** to reduce tax overall

🧠 This allows for **strategic tax planning** that’s not possible as a sole trader.

---

### 3. **Retain Profits in the Business**

Unlike a sole trader (where all income is personal income), a company can:

- Keep profits in the business without distributing them
- Defer personal tax until you draw a salary or dividend
- Use retained earnings for reinvestment (e.g., R&D, marketing, hiring)

📌 Helpful if you're building a startup or want to **avoid jumping tax brackets** personally.

---

### 4. **Superannuation Contributions Are Deductible**

Paying yourself a salary from your company allows:

- **Tax-deductible super contributions**
- Access to **employer-level benefits** like fringe benefits (car, laptop, etc.)
- Structured personal retirement planning

---

### 5. **Asset Protection (Indirection)**

While not a direct tax benefit, separating yourself legally protects your personal assets if the company incurs debts, gets sued, or loses money.

You can also:

- Use holding companies or trusts for asset protection
- Segregate high-risk activities

---

### 6. **R&D Tax Incentives**

If you’re doing software or tech development:

- Companies can access **R&D tax offsets**, often **43.5% refundable**
- This is only available through companies (not sole traders)

---

### 7. **More Options for Investment or Equity Compensation**

If you plan to:

- Raise capital
- Issue shares or options
- Bring in co-founders or employees with equity

You **must** be a company. This also opens the door for **ESIC** tax incentives (Early Stage Innovation Company) for your investors.

---

## ⚠️ But Be Aware:

- You’ll need to lodge both **company tax returns** and **personal returns**
- Must pay **ASIC fees** (~$290/year) and comply with director obligations
- Likely need **an accountant** for setup and compliance (~$1k–2k/year)

---

## 🧠 When Does a Company Structure Make Sense?

| Scenario | Recommendation |
| --- | --- |
| Income **>$120k/year** | ✅ Consider company |
| Want to raise capital / issue equity | ✅ Must be a company |
| Want to **retain profits** for growth | ✅ Consider company |
| Primarily freelancing under $100k | ❌ Stick with sole trader |
| Claiming R&D tax offsets | ✅ Must be a company |
| Want to split income with others | ✅ Company allows this |
| Want to minimise admin | ❌ Sole trader is simpler |
