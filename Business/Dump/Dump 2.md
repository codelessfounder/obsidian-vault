Project switch checklist:

### **Project Switch Checklist**

### 🧭 Terminal Context

- [ ] `cd` into the correct project folder
- [ ] Confirm the terminal is not still pointing at the previous repo
- [ ] (Optional) Run `git status` to verify you’re on the right branch and project

### 🌐 Dev Server

- [ ] Kill any running dev server from the previous project (`Ctrl+C`)
- [ ] Choose a unique port if needed (`-port 3001`, `PORT=3001 npm run dev`)
- [ ] Run `npm install` if it’s your first time in the repo

### 📦 Environment Variables

- [ ] Load the correct `.env` file (automatically happens if present in root)
- [ ] Double-check for variable name collisions (e.g. shared `SUPABASE_URL`)
- [ ] If you use `nvm`, run `nvm use` to match project’s Node version (`.nvmrc`)

### 💻 Editor & Git Hygiene

- [ ] Close any leftover tabs from the other project to avoid confusion
- [ ] Make sure Git commits/branches are being made in the correct repo
- [ ] Ensure `.gitignore` is respected and not showing temp files from old builds

### ⚙️ Package Manager Consistency

- [ ] Stick with the same one (`npm`, `yarn`, or `pnpm`) per project
- [ ] Delete `node_modules` if switching managers between sessions

### 🧠 System Resources

- [ ] If the system is laggy, close other project windows or tabs
- [ ] Watch for port conflicts or EMFILE errors on large projects

### 🐳 Backend Services (if used)

- [ ] Shut down any containers from the previous project (`docker-compose down`)
- [ ] Avoid port conflicts (e.g. Postgres, Redis, Supabase emulator)
- [ ] Use project-specific volumes/networks if needed

SAFE raising rhetoric:



| **Investor Question** | **Founder Answer (What You Say)** |
| --- | --- |
| **Why are you raising on a SAFE instead of equity?** | “At this stage, setting a precise valuation is premature and slows things down. A SAFE lets us move quickly, stay focused on building, and reward you with a cap/discount for backing us early.” |
| **What’s the valuation cap, and why that number?** | “We set the cap based on where we see comparable companies at our stage and the milestones we’ll hit before a Series A. It gives you upside protection if we grow faster than expected.” |
| **How much SAFE capital are you planning to raise?** | “We’re targeting $X in total SAFEs before converting. We’re being deliberate about the size so there’s no messy overhang when we raise the next priced round.” |
| **What happens if you raise more SAFEs after mine? Won’t I get diluted?** | “All SAFEs convert at the same valuation cap or better, so you won’t be disadvantaged. We’re transparent about the total SAFE pool, and our goal is to keep it clean and limited.” |
| **What ownership will I actually end up with?** | “It depends on the Series A valuation, but your SAFE cap ensures you’ll convert into equity at no worse than $X valuation, which is typically founder-friendly while still fair to early investors.” |
| **Why should I invest now instead of waiting for the priced round?** | “Because your SAFE gives you either a discount or the cap — meaning you’re rewarded for conviction today. If you wait, you’ll invest at a higher valuation without those protections.” |
| **Do I get pro rata rights?** | “SAFEs don’t include them by default, but we can provide a side letter so you’ll have the ability to participate in future rounds.” |
| **Aren’t SAFEs risky for investors?** | “They’re standardised and YC-backed. The terms are simple and clean, and your downside is no different from equity at this stage — you’re betting on the team and traction. The upside is, you’re locked in at a favourable cap.” |
| **What’s stopping you from stacking tons of SAFEs?** | “Nothing in the legal docs, which is why we’re being upfront: we’re only raising $X on SAFEs before converting to equity. That way everyone has clarity and confidence in ownership.” |
| **Why not just do a priced round now?** | “A priced round means higher legal fees, board seats, and heavier dilution before we’ve proven enough. SAFEs let us delay that until Series A, when we’ll have the traction to justify stronger terms for everyone.” |

## Example: raising 5M SAFE:



### **Scenario Setup**

- Total raised: **$5M**
- Number of investors: **10**
- Check size each: **$500k**
- All SAFEs on the **same terms** (same cap, same discount, etc.)

---

### **What That Means for Investors**

- **Conversion mechanics**: Each investor’s $500k SAFE will convert into equity at the Series A once you do a priced round.
- If you set, say, a **$20M valuation cap**, each $500k would buy:20M500k=2.5% of the company (pre-Series A)
500k20M=2.5% of the company (pre-Series A)\frac{500k}{20M} = 2.5\% \text{ of the company (pre-Series A)}
- With 10 investors, that’s **25% of the company** allocated across the SAFE pool once they convert.

---

### **The SAFE Pool (“Overhang”)**

- Until the priced round, these 10 investors don’t technically own equity — they’re just owed future equity.
- When you raise a Series A, all 10 SAFEs convert at the agreed cap (or discount).
- This creates a **large dilution event** at Series A, since the equity you’ve promised gets crystallised all at once.

---

### **How Founders Frame This**

- **Clarity**: “We’ve capped the SAFE pool at $5M, so you know your eventual % ownership isn’t being diluted by endless SAFEs.”
- **Fairness**: “All investors are on the same terms — no side deals, no last-minute changes.”
- **Future fundraising**: “This conversion will set us up cleanly for a Series A without overhang confusion.”

---

### **Key Considerations**

28. **Cap Level Matters** — At a $20M cap, the SAFE pool is 25%. At a $40M cap, it’s ~12.5%.
29. **Stacking Risk** — If you raise another SAFE later, all investors share more dilution; investors will ask if $5M is the *final* SAFE amount.
30. **Series A Optics** — Some VCs dislike walking into a round where 25%+ of the company is already allocated to SAFEs (they call it “too much overhang”).
31. **Pro Rata** — If you gave side letters, each of the 10 investors may also expect to participate in the Series A.

---

👉 So in your example, if you raised **$5M on SAFEs with a $20M cap**, you’ve effectively sold **~25% of the company already**, spread evenly across 10 investors. They don’t “own” it yet, but the Series A will crystalise that ownership.

Dividends and profit splits in a company:

“””In a high-growth startup, **profits are rarely "split" in the early years.** Instead, they are reinvested into the company to fuel user acquisition and scale. However, because you are building a sweepstakes business—which can be "cash-flow heavy"—you need a plan for when the company actually has excess cash.
In the 51/49 or 55/45 dynamic we discussed, here is how the money actually moves:**
1. The Hierarchy of Payments**
Before anyone "splits" a profit, the company must meet its obligations. In a professional startup, the order of cash flow looks like this:
1. **Operating Expenses (OpEx):** AWS/Supabase bills, OpenAI API costs, legal retainers, and prize fulfillment.
2. **Market-Rate Salaries:** Once the company is making money, both you and the CMO should draw a salary. This is better for taxes and prevents one person from feeling like they are "funding" the other's life.
3. **The "War Chest" (Retained Earnings):** You keep 3–6 months of runway in the bank for marketing spikes or legal emergencies.
4. **Distributions (The "Profit Split"):** Only after the above are met do you consider taking money out.**
2. How the Split Works (Dividends)**
If you decide to take a profit distribution (called a **Dividend**), the law (and your Operating Agreement) typically dictates that it must be **Pro-Rata**.
• **If you own 55% and he owns 45%:** If you decide to distribute €10,000 in profit, you *must* take €5,500 and he *must* take €4,500. You cannot easily change this split without creating a tax nightmare or legal "breach of fiduciary duty."
• **The Trap:** If you give him 50% now, you are locked into giving him 50% of every spare Euro the company ever makes, even if you are doing 90% of the work three years from now.**
3. Handling the "Salary vs. Profit" Tension**
This is where most co-founder fights happen.
• **The Engineer's View:** "We should keep the money in the company to build more features."
• **The CMO's View:** "I need to spend this money on influencers to get more users."
The Solution: The Board Vote
Even in a 2-person company, you act as the "Board."
• Since you have **51%**, you have the final say on the **Budget**.
• You can decide that "The company will not pay dividends this year; all profit will be used for a 51/49 split of the new R&D budget."**
4. Better Alternatives for a "Full-Time" CMO**
If the CMO needs cash to live on but you want to keep the equity split uneven, use these tools:
• **Preferred Salary:** You can agree that he gets a slightly higher monthly salary in the beginning because he put in the 30k cash, while you keep the higher equity.
• **Bonus Structure:** Instead of splitting *all* profits, create a "Performance Bonus."
    ◦ *Example:* "If we hit €50k profit this month, the CMO gets a €2k bonus for hitting his CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) targets." This keeps him hungry without giving away the long-term equity.**

My Recommendation**

2. **Pay yourselves equal salaries** once profitable.
