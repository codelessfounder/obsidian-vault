When deciding whether an app should be a web app or a mobile app, it depends on several factors such as user experience, functionality, target audience, and accessibility. Here's a general guideline:

### **Web Apps** (best suited for):

1. **Content Management**: Apps that handle content (e.g., blogs, news sites, and content-rich platforms) where users are likely to access it from desktops or laptops.
2. **Complex or Data-Intensive Applications**: Apps that require heavy data input/output, like CRM tools, enterprise resource planning (ERP) systems, or dashboards (e.g., Watchcrow).
3. **Collaborative Platforms**: Apps that allow multiple users to interact with or edit data simultaneously, like project management tools (Trello, Slack).
4. **Cross-Platform Accessibility**: If you want your app to be accessible across different platforms (Windows, macOS, Linux) without needing separate native apps for each.
5. **SEO/Discovery**: Apps where being found on search engines is crucial (e.g., online stores, blogs, service listings).

### **Mobile Apps** (best suited for):

6. **Location-Based Services**: Apps like maps, ridesharing, or local discovery tools that rely on GPS or mobile device features.
7. **Push Notifications**: Apps that benefit from timely push notifications (e.g., messaging apps, weather apps, and news apps).
8. **Offline Access**: Apps that need to work without a constant internet connection (e.g., note-taking apps, navigation apps).
9. **Camera/Device Integration**: Apps that rely heavily on the device's camera, sensors, or accelerometer (e.g., augmented reality apps, photo/video apps).
10. **On-the-Go Usage**: Apps that need to be accessible and easy to use in mobile environments, such as social media, fitness tracking, and shopping.

### **Hybrid Approach**:

Some apps may benefit from a combination of both, offering a responsive web app alongside a mobile app, each optimized for its platform's strengths (e.g., social networks, banking apps).
