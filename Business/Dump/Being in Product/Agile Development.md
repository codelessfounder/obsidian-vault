*Lean Startup p130*

“Sprints”:

- One month iteration cycles
- Within the sprints, write a series of *user stories *(agile development technique)

Why is a user story so beneficial in the iteration process?

- Writing a story to describe a feature from the POV of a customer helps engineers focus on the customer’s perspective throughout the development process
- It helps massively with prioritisation of workloads
- Typically, batch sizes of work are only a day or two, which allow teams to change direction quickly and be highly responsive to changes in the business requirements of the product owner
- ***THIS is the crux of agile development***

The problem with agile development…

*teams are able to quickly release new product features and collect customer feedback in the form of anecdotes and interviews that gives some indication of whether the customers like the new features*

- This may show a certain amount of data that indicated improvement e.g. total number of customers increasing, number of returning customers increasing
- BUT how do you know if the numbers increasing were actually caused by their development efforts?

It brings it back to the very important question of:

“How do you know that the features that you’re building are contributing to the growth metrics of the company?”

“How do you know the prioritisation decisions of the Founder actually make sense?”

Age old startup questions:

1. *“How do we know which features of prioritise?”*
2. *“How can we get more customers to sign up and pay?”*
3. *“How can we get the word out about our product?”*

The grand bargain of agile development:

Engineers agree to adapt the product to the business’s constantly changing requirements but are not responsible for the quality of those business decisions.

- This makes *agile development *efficient from the POV of developers - it allows them to stay focused on creating features and technical design.
    - If there is an attempt to introduce the need to learn in that process, productivity could be undermined.

Its not enough to loosely follow the lean startup learning milestones:

- (Shipping an early product, establishing baseline metrics and demonstrating short iteration cycles)

^prioritising the wrong types of metrics of being inconsistent with the the types of metrics you track will mean that founders will not be able to draw clear cause and effect inferences from their efforts. Prioritizing work effectively is extremely challenging.


**Kanban**

*Lean Startup p138-*

Kanban is a lean manufacturing principle that constrains he capacity of features being worked on in the product prioritisation process\

Layout:

| Backlog | In Progress | Built | Validated |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
|   | G | D | F |
| H -> | B-> | E |   |
| I -> | C-> | A |   |

- F is validated
- B and C have been built, but under Kanban cannot be moved to the next column until A, D & E have been validated
- Work cannot begin on the new features H & I until there is space in the “in progress” column.

Each of the letter are features/user stories

User stories cannot be considered complete until they have led to validated learning

There are four states of development:

4. In the product backlog
5. Actively being built
6. Done (coding completed)
7. Process of validation

Kanban only permits 3 stories in each of the columns

Stories cannot progress if the next column is full

When a story is validated, is can be removed form the Kanban board

If the validation fails, the respective feature/user story is removed from the product

Definition of validated learning: “*Knowing whether the story was a good idea to have been done in the first place.”*

- Validation will usually come in the form of a split test showing the change in consumer behaviour
- May also come in the form customer interviews or surveys, but these are less impactful

Kanban is counter-intuitive, especially for engineers:

- Unless features become validated, engineers are not able to move on to building new features
- This means that engineers must engage in non-engineering efforts to investigate the validity of some of the features they’ve built
    - e.g. talking to customers, looking at split test data.

A learning process for teams:

8. Initially, engineers see this as a big batch of work followed by extensive testing and validation
9. As engineers look for ways to increase their productivity, they start to find that including validation exercises from the beginning, the whole team can be more productive.


**Validated Learning**

- being able to demonstrate progress while living through the uncertain life of growing a startup
- Empirically demonstrating that you have discovered valuable truths about your startups present and future business prospects

Benefits:

10. It is more accurate & faster than market forecasting or classical business planning
11. It it the antidote to “achieving failure” (successfully executing a plan that leads to nowhere)

**Cohort & Split Tests**

*Lean Startup, p136*

Startups can change the way they evaluate success in two ways:

12. Switching from measuring gross metrics to cohort-based metrics
13. Switching from looking for cause and effect relationships after the fact to launching features a *true split-test experiments*.

Split test experiment: where different versions of the product are offered to customers at the same time.

Benefits of split testing:

14. by observing changes in behaviour between groups, founders can make inferences about the impact of different product variations.
15. Founders can distinguish between features that seem better in the eyes of engineers and designers versus features that actually have a positive impact on the customer’s experience
16. Allows teams to re-define their understanding of what customers want and don’t want.

Difficulty of implementation:

17. Split testing requires extra accounting and metrics to keep track of each variation

The silver lining:

- In the long run, split tests will save a tremendous amount of time by eliminating work that doesn’t matter.

After all of this, startups and founders can say they have establish real validated learning:

*“After running a series fo split tests, we found that customers care most about the types of photos and images they can see on their authenticity report of their watch”*

**Innovation Accounting**

*Lean Startup, Eric Reis, Chapter 7, p114-*

A startup’s job is to:

18. Rigorously measure where it is right now and confront the hard truths that the assessment reveals
19. Devise experiments to learn how to move the real numbers closer to the ideal reflected in the business plan

*The myth of perseverance is so dangerous because we want to keep believing in our ideas even when the writing is on the wall*

Startups are too unpredictable for forecast and milestones to be accurate.

Questions to ask startups when you meet with them for the first time:

20. A*re you making your product better?* (They will always answer yes)
21. How do you know?

Reality: Most answers will be along the lines of “we made a number of changes last month and our customers seemed to like them, and our overall numbers are higher this month, so we must be on the right track.

- Hitting a product milestone, talking to a few customers and seeing if the numbers go up are NOT an indicator of whether a startup is making good progress

Innovation Accounting:

Innovation accounting enables startups to prove objectively that they are learning how to grow a sustainable business.

It begins by turning the *leap of faith assumptions * into a quantitative financial model.

Two examples of financial models:

22. Established manufacturing company
    - Rate of growth depends on 3 things:
        - The profitability of each customer
        - The cost of acquiring new customers
        - The repeat purchase rate of existing customers
23. A Marketplace
    - Success depends on the engine of growth of network effects that make it the premier destination for both buyers and sellers to transact business
    - It is important to measure that the network effects are working (high retention rate of new buyers and sellers)

The three learning milestones of innovation accounting:

24. Use an MVP to establish real data on where the company is right now
25. Attempt to tune the engine from the baseline towards the ideal
26. Pivot or persevere

Establish a baseline:

- Done through the MVP
- The MVP will test most of the startup’s assumptions and establish baseline metrics for each assumption simultaneously
- A startup can also build separate MVPs that are aimed on getting feedback on one assumption at a time
- MVPs are the first tangible example of a startups *learning milestone*
- An MVP allows a startup to fill in real baseline data in its growth model - conversion rates, sign-up and trial rates, customer LTV etc
- It makes sense to test the riskiest assumptions first

Tuning the engine:

- Every product development, marketing, or other initiative that a startup undertakes should be targeted at improving one of the drivers of its growth model
    - E.g. Spending time improving the design of the product to **make it easer for new customers to use**.
        - This suggests that the *activation rate (registered users) *is a driver of growth and needs to be increased to get closer to the ideal
        - To demonstrate *validated learning*, the design changes must improve the activation rate of new customers. If they do not, the new design should be judged as a failure.
        - *A good design is one that changes customer behaviour for the better*

Pivot or persevere:

- The opposite of seeing the numbers in a startup’s model rise towards the ideal from the baseline metrics established at the MVP is when the the ideal recess further into the distance and metrics go down.
- Even the greatest storytellers won’t be able to cover up this fact: *if we’re not moving the drivers of our business model, we’re not making progress. This is a sign to pivot.*

Funnel metrics critical to our engine of growth:

- Customer registration
- Application download rate
- Trial
- Repeat usage
- Purchase

Cohort Analysis:

- One of the most important tools of startup analytics
- Looking at the performance of each group of customers that comes into contact with the product independently
- Different groups:
    - *Registered but didn’t log in*
    - *Logged in*
    - *Had one conversation*
    - Had five conversations
    - *Paid*
- ^ these are all engine of growth metrics
- They allow us to understand a business quantitatively and have much more predictive power than traditional gross metrics

Optimisation versus learning:

Optimisation in traditional business (such as AB testing) do not work the same for startups & specifically product improvement.

- *If you are building the wrong thing, optimising the product is pointless*
- Startup’s progress has to be measure against a high bar: evidence that a sustainable business can be built around its products or services. That’s a standard that can only be assessed **if a startup has made clear, tangible assumptions ahead of time.**

Implementing real learning milestones prevents the likely possibility that the company is executing a plan that does not make sense.

**A word of warning: **Companies of any size can come to rely on the wrong kind of metrics to guide their actions. This is what tempts founders and CEOs to start buying a the usual “theatre tricks” such as las-minute ad buys, channel stuffing, whiz-bang demos in a desperate attempt to make their number look better (vanity metrics).

The innovation accounting framework makes it clear when the company is stuck and needs to change direction.

*Only 5% of entrepreneurship is the big idea, the business model, the whiteboard strategising and the splitting up of the spoils. The other 95% is the gritty work that is measure by innovation accounting: product prioritisation decisions, deciding which customers to target or listen to, and having the courage to subject a grand vision to constant testing and feedback.*


**Pivot or Persevere**

*Lean startup, p149*

The main question:

***Are we making sufficient progress to believe that our original strategic hypothesis is correct, or do we need to make a major change?***

- It should be noted that human elements such as vision, judgement & intuition are inevitable here. There is no way to remove them.

Pivot definition: a  structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis about the product, strategy and engine of growth.

*There is no bigger destroyer of creative potential than the misguided decision to persevere.*

Startup productivity:

- Fundamentally, it is about aligning our efforts with a business and product that are working to create value and drive growth.
- Successful pivots put us on a path toward growing a sustainable business

“Getting stuck in the land of the living dead”

- When a company has achieved a mediocre amount of success but is not living up to the expectations of its founders and investors.

The earlier that a startup conducts techniques of innovation accounting and hypothesis testing, the sooner & easier it is to make a pivot.

Also, using actionable metrics to assess each of your leap of faith assumptions makes it easier to see failure.

FAILURE IS A PREREQUISITE TO LEARNING.

*The problem with the notion of shipping a product and then seeing what happens is that you are guaranteed to succeed at just that - seeing what happens.*

Look for recurring patters in the feedback you get from customers.

Each MVP should be created & validated in a shorter timeframe:

e.g:

First MVP/hypothesis: 8 months

Second MVP/hypothesis: 4 months

Third MVP/Hypothesis: 3 months

Fourth Hypothesis: 1 month

Note: it is very tempting to credit the acceleration of growth to the product development work that has been going on.

A legacy product:

- A product previously built that no longer serves the new goals of the company
- Much of a product must be discarded when the startup pivots into testing new hypothesis

*A startup’s runway is the number of pivots it can still make:*

- When startups start to run low on cash, they can extend their runway in two ways:
27. Cutting costs
28. Raising additional funds
- Note: if entrepreneurs cut costs indiscriminately, they are as liable to cut the costs that are allowing the company to get through its *build-measure-learn *feedback loop as they are to cut waste
    - If the cut results in a slow down of the feedback loop, all they have accomplished is to help the startup die more slowly

A true measure of runway:

- The number of opportunities it has left to make a fundamental change to its business strategy

Looking through the lens of pivots rather than time allows founders to have another outlook on extending their runway: get to each pivot faster.

- The startup needs to find ways to achieve the same amount of validated learning at Lowe cost or in a shorter time.

Pivots Require Courage:

- Many entrepreneurs are afraid. Acknowledging failure can lead to dangerously low morale.
- **Most entrepreneurs’ biggest fear is the thought their vision might be deemed wrong without being given a real chance to prove itself**
    - This fear drives much of the resistance to the MVP, split-testing, and other techniques to test hypotheses. It also drives up the risk because testing doesn’t occur until the vision is fully represented - but by that time, it is often too late to pivot and time & funding has ran out.
- Entrepreneurs need to be willing to fail - often & in a public way.

Ignore the fear & focus on what the customers actually say. Humbly test your theories, get feedback, iterate to see what the market really thinks. Listen to feedback honestly. Continue to innovate in directions you think will create meaning in the world.

The Pivot or Persevere meeting:

Telltale signs of the need to pivot:

29. The decreasing effectiveness of product effectiveness towards proving your hypothesis
30. General feeling that product development should be more productive

Have a regular *pivot or persevere* meeting. Every 2 months.

- The product development team must bring a complete report of the results of its product optimisation efforts over time(not just the past period) as well as a comparison of how those results stack up against expectations
- The business leadership should bring detailed accounts of their conversations with past, current and potential customers.

Talk to industry professionals

*Making the courageous decision to pivot should be celebrated. If you had never launched the current (yet failed) product, your team would have never learned what it needed to know to pivot.*

Pivot repurposing:

- It is not usually necessary to throw out EVERYTHING that came before the pivot and start over.
- Instead, it is about repurposing what as been built based on what has been learned to find a more positive direction towards a sustainable business model.

Low-quality MVP to Mainstream product:

- The rationale for building a low-quality MVP is that developing any features beyond what early adopters require is a form of waste
- HOWEVER, this logic will only take you so far:
    - Once you have found success with early adopters, you want to sell to mainstream customers
    - The problem here is that mainstream customers have different requirements and are much more demanding
- A *customer segment pivot *is required. This means realising the existing product solves a problem for real customers but not the customers it originally planned to serve.
    - This is a partial confirmation of your hypothesis, but requires a pivot.

What is the goal?

**Testing a clear hypothesis in the service of the company’s vision**

Pivots are the heart of the lean startup model. It is what makes companies resilient in the face of mistakes: if we are wrong, we must realise it and find the agility to take another path.

*a pivot is a special kind of structured change designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis about the product, business model and engine of growth.*

**Lean Startup Summary:**

*The lean startup is a book by Eric Reis inspired by processes originating in “lean manufacturing”. Reis focuses on principles such as “validated learning”, scientific experimentation & processes that would seem counter-intuitive to regular business practices, but lead into shortening development cycles, measuring actual process without resorting to vanity metrics, ultimately finding what customers really want. The benefit of these methods allows startups to be agile in their strategy, learning & development*

Startup definition: ***a human institution designed to create a new product or service under conditions of extreme uncertainty***

Chapter 3: Validated Learning:

- Validated learning is a rigorous for demonstrating process. It is a fundamental element of the startup journey
- It is the anthesis of “after-the-fact rationalisation” and good story telling that is designed to hide failure
- It is the process fo demonstrating empirically that your team has discovered valuable truths about your startups present an future business prospects
    - Startups discover more about their customer so they can create a product that directly solves customer issues
- Learning is the unit of measurement in startups.
    - Empirical data collected from real customers allows for validated learning to make positive improvements to the startups core metrics
- Every startup in every industry is a grand experiment.
- The question isn’t “can this be built?”, the question should be “can this product be built and can we build a sustainable business around this set of products and services?”
- Find synthesis between your product vision and what customers will accept
- True startup productivity:
    - Systematically figuring out the right things to build
        - Every product, every feature, every marketing campaign is understood to be an experiment designed to learn something about your customers
- *Lean thinking: Value that provides benefit to the customer. Anything else is a waste.*
    - *In a startup, who the customer is and what the customer might find valuable are unknown. This is the uncertainty that is an essential part of the definition of a startup.*

Case Study:

*Hewlett Packard Volunteer Program: Validated learning as a transferrable skill to large enterprises*

Caroline Barlein. Head of Global Community Involvement @ HP.

Despite being part of a global enterprise with 300,000 employees, Caroline is an entrepreneur in her own right.

Caroline is tasked with working to get more employees to take advantage of the company’s policy on volunteering.

Policy: 4hrs of employee company time dedicated to volunteering in his/her community

Problem: Most employees don’t know about the policy & only a tiny fraction of people take advantage of it. Because of HPs employees’ diverse skillset, they have the potential to make huge change within the community

*This isn’t a startup by conventional definition, *but startup frameworks can be applied to scenario that would have otherwise been handled with traditional business management practice

The extreme uncertainties that Caroline faces & the assumptions she has:

53. HP has never had large-scale adoption of a volunteering program before
54. Caroline doesn’t know the real reasons why people aren’t volunteering
55. How does she know how to change the behaviour of hundreds of thousands of people in 170 countries?
56. Caroline’s goal is to inspire her colleagues to make the world a better place

This is a clear list of untested assumptions.

Strategy to test these assumptions:

57. Identify the value hypothesis: What’s a good indicator that employees find donating their time valuable?
58. Experiment
    - What could serve as a proxy for the value participants were gaining from volunteering?
        - e.g. how many sign up for volunteering again? Employees re-investing their time is a strong indicator they find the program valuable
59. Identify the growth hypothesis: How will the program spread from the early adopter to gain mass adoption throughout the company?
- This could happen through viral growth. Will the early participants actively spread the word to other employees?
60. Experiment again
- e.g. take a small number of existing long-term employees and provide an exceptional volunteer opportunity for them.
61. *Concierge MVP*
- Caroline could provide a experience/service to a small group of employees as best she can manually, that aligns directly with her vision. Doing this would allow her to properly measure the behaviour of the employees:
    - How many of the volunteers complete their volunteer assignments?
    - How many volunteer a second time?
    - How many are willing to recruit a colleague to participate in a subsequent volunteer activity?

What is the benefit of this ‘lean startup’ approach versus traditional business management & strategic planning?

62. Experiments & learning can be conducted in a matter of weeks, 10x faster than the strategic planning process
63. It can happen in parallel with strategic planning whilst the plan is still being formulated
64. Even when the experiments are massive failures, these results can be instrumental in deciding future strategy
- E.g. What is no volunteers could be found that aligned with the hypothesis in the business plan? Then the team has successfully learned it is time to pivot their strategy and saved the company months in planning.
