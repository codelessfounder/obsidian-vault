When a tech company is acquired for around **$100 million**, the metrics can vary significantly based on factors like the industry, growth stage, and the acquirer’s strategic intent. However, here are some typical financial and operational numbers you might expect from a tech company at that valuation:

**1. Revenue and Growth Metrics**

•	**Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR):**

•	SaaS companies: $8M–$15M ARR

•	Non-recurring revenue models (e.g., e-commerce): $20M–$40M

•	Expected growth rate: 30%–100% YoY, depending on the sector.

•	**Multiple of ARR/Revenue:**

•	Typical acquisition multiples: 6x–12x ARR for high-growth SaaS.

•	Lower multiples (3x–5x) if growth is slowing or in less dynamic sectors.

**Profitability Metrics**

•	**EBITDA or Net Profit:**

•	Many high-growth tech startups may not be profitable, focusing on growth instead.

•	If profitable, EBITDA might range between **$1M–$5M**.

•	**Burn Rate:**

•	If not profitable, the burn rate (monthly losses) could be $500K–$1M, with several months or years of runway left from previous funding rounds.

**User and Customer Metrics**

•	**Active Users or Subscribers:**

•	SaaS: 5K–30K paying users, depending on pricing.

•	Consumer apps: 1M–5M monthly active users (MAUs).

•	E-commerce: $50M+ annual GMV (Gross Merchandise Volume).

•	**Customer Lifetime Value (LTV) / Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) ratio:**

•	A solid LTV:CAC ratio is **3:1** or higher.

**Team Size and Talent**

•	**Employee Count:**

•	Typically **50–150 employees**, depending on the business model.

•	Acquisitions often value engineering or product talent highly, which can drive up the valuation even without massive revenue numbers.

**Strategic Value and Market Position**

•	**Strategic Fit:**

•	Acquirers may pay a premium if the company fills a product gap or offers valuable IP (intellectual property).

•	Market position and brand reputation also play a role—being a leader in a niche or having proprietary tech can increase the valuation.

•	**Intellectual Property (IP):**

•	Patents, proprietary algorithms, or strong tech differentiators can justify a higher multiple even with modest revenues.

**Example Scenarios for a $100M Acquisition**

1.	**SaaS Product:**

•	$10M ARR, growing 60% YoY, break-even or slightly negative cash flow.

2.	**E-commerce Brand:**

•	$25M annual revenue, 15% EBITDA margin, growing 40% YoY.

3.	**Consumer App:**

•	2M MAUs, modest monetization (~$8M revenue), but valuable user data and product fit for the acquirer.

In summary, a $100M acquisition target would likely be growing quickly with promising metrics, although not necessarily profitable. The valuation often reflects **future potential and strategic value** more than just current financials.
