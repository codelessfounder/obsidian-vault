### Consolidated notes

Principles of product discovery:

1. Design and technology are inherently intertwined.
2. Most ideas fail; good ideas require iteration.
3. Validate ideas with real customers and users.
4. Validate quickly and cheaply.
5. Test feasibility before full discovery output.
6. Test value before full build.
7. Test business viability before full build.
8. Experience customer pain together and share learnings generously.
9. MVPs should behave like prototypes for learning, not polished production products.

Prototype principles:

1. Product discovery should find the fastest and cheapest learning path before full build.
2. Prototypes force deeper problem understanding and surface major issues earlier.
3. Prototypes improve cross-functional alignment and shared understanding.
4. Prototypes reduce feasibility, value, usability, and business viability risk.

Testing demand:

1. Fake-door test: present a potential feature and invite customer conversations.
2. Landing-page test: explain the potential offering and collect interest.

Testing for value (quantitative):

- Quant measures what users do; qual explains why.
- Use A/B testing, optimization testing, and invite-only testing where relevant.
- Instrument basic usage analytics for every new feature so value signals are measurable.
- Prioritize statistically significant evidence over assumptions.

Testing for business viability:

- Product must satisfy business constraints, not just usability/feasibility/value.
- Validate model viability early: delivery cost must remain below sustainable revenue.
- Pressure-test constraints with Marketing, Sales, Customer Success, Finance, Legal, BD, Security, and executive leadership.
- Avoid shipping products that violate core business/reputation constraints.

Analytics testing for product success:

1. User behavior analytics
2. Business analytics (click rates, conversion, retention/LTV)
3. Financial analytics (ASP, billings, time-to-close, operating cost)
4. GTM analytics (acquisition spend, sales program cost, NPS/surveys)

Cloud tooling and web analytics make this tracking easier to implement consistently.

Prototype types:

- User testing prototype: low-fidelity workflow representation for user validation.
- Live data prototype: focused on collecting usage evidence for major risk questions.
- Hybrid ("Wizard of Oz") prototype: high-fidelity UX with manual backstage operations before automation.

Feasibility prototypes:

- Built by engineers to reduce technical delivery risk (performance, scalability, algorithm, integration, and legacy constraints).
- Intended as throwaway code to answer feasibility questions quickly.
- Typical duration is 1-2 days unless new technology risk is unusually high.

Testing for product-market fit:

- Use the Sean Ellis test with core-value users.
- Ask: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"
- If >40% say "very disappointed," PMF is likely emerging.

Communicating product learnings:

- Every 1-2 weeks, the head of product should spend 15-30 minutes sharing major discovery learnings.
- Focus on what worked, what failed, and what teams will test next.
- Share failures explicitly so teams can adjust faster.
- Be generous with knowledge sharing across teams.

*“Customers don’t have to buy products and users don’t have to choose a feature… They do so when they perceive real value; the customer must understand your product to be better than what they are currently using.”*

*“The biggest loss of all when building new products is the opportunity cost of what the startup could and should have been building instead. You can’t get that time back.” Marty Cagan*

It is not enough to have similar features to your competitors. Your product must be substantially better so as to motivate a customer to switch from a competitors product to yours.

The reality is: No one has any idea about whether the business ideas are going work. It’s impossible to know. You must come to terms with the fact that half of your ideas are just not going to work.
